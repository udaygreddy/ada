The problem

Implementation associates are legally barred from client systems. Today, collecting a client's payroll history, tax filings, and employee records means weeks of email back-and-forth — chasing files, receiving the wrong quarter, asking again.

ADA moves the work to where the access already exists: the client's own AI assistant. ADP ships instructions, not a connection. The client's assistant does the collecting — guided, checked, and recorded at every step.

What ADP receives

  • The documents, organized by category
  • Manifest — what was collected, from where, with validation verdicts
  • Gap report — exactly what's still missing, and where to get it
  • Audit ledger — the complete, tamper-evident consent record

Why it can be trusted

Zero access

ADP ships a reviewable skill — plain instructions and small open scripts. No service, no login, no data path back to ADP.

reviewable · no dependencies

Consent enforced in code

Two approval gates are enforced by scripts, not by the AI's good behavior. A file without a client-approval token cannot enter the package.

gate 1: source · gate 2: per file

Tamper-evident record

Every authorization, approval, and verdict is written to a hash-chained ledger. Alter any entry and the chain breaks — visibly.

sha-256 · append-only

PII guarded

SSNs and account numbers are detected in code, masked before AI review, and sensitive files are never auto-included.

flag · mask · confirm

What this changes

Faster starts

Collection happens in one guided session instead of weeks of email rounds — and validation catches wrong-quarter files before they reach ADP.

Implementation sees the gaps

The gap report tells the team exactly which items exist, which are missing, and where each one lives — before the first follow-up call.

Compliance built in

The legal boundary isn't a policy promise — it's the architecture. The audit ledger documents that the client approved every access and every file.
